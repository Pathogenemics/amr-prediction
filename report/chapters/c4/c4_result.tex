\section{Experimental Setting}

% Y doan: dat khung danh gia va noi ro nhung gi duoc giu co dinh qua moi thi nghiem.
% Vai tro: xac dinh rang cac khac biet ket qua chu yeu den tu connection scope, khong phai do cohort hay workflow thay doi.
All experiments use the same shared cleaned genotype-phenotype cohort, the same phenotype-processing and genotype-normalization pipeline, and the same antibiotic-specific prepared-input workflow described in Chapter~3. The machine-learning experiments also keep the same one-model-per-antibiotic strategy and the same five-fold stratified cross-validation protocol across all settings. The project repository contains the workflow code, prepared inputs, and result files used for the experiments reported in this chapter\footnote{\url{https://github.com/Pathogenemics/amr-prediction}}. Under this shared framework, performance differences reflect how genotype evidence is connected to phenotype under different biological scopes rather than changes in cohort construction, preprocessing, or training configuration.

% Y doan: liet ke pham vi thuc nghiem va cac quy uoc danh gia.
% Vai tro: giup nguoi doc biet ro cac bai toan cu the duoc danh gia va cach doc metric trong ca chuong.
The final experiment set contains 14 antibiotics: amoxicillin-clavulanic acid, ampicillin, cefoxitin, ceftiofur, ceftriaxone, chloramphenicol, ciprofloxacin, gentamicin, kanamycin, nalidixic acid, streptomycin, sulfisoxazole, tetracycline, and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. Throughout the chapter, \texttt{resistant} is treated as the positive class. Result interpretation follows the final priority order of recall first, precision second, and ROC-AUC third. Accuracy is reported only as supporting context because class imbalance can make it appear strong even when resistant-isolate detection remains weak.

% Y doan: giai thich cac setting duoc so sanh trong chuong.
% Vai tro: phan biet so sanh ML theo scope voi baseline rule-based va noi ro vi sao rule-based chi co strict/broad.
The machine-learning comparison contains three scope settings, namely \textit{strict ML}, \textit{broad ML}, and \textit{all ML}. The deterministic baselines are \textit{strict rule-based} and \textit{broad rule-based}. No rule-based \textit{all} baseline is reported because the \textit{all} scope intentionally retains determinants without requiring direct biological relevance to the target antibiotic, so it no longer defines an antibiotic-specific deterministic matching rule.

\section{Antibiotic-Specific Scope Comparison}

Because one model is trained for each antibiotic and determinant relevance varies across drugs, scope effects are interpreted primarily at the antibiotic level. Scope comparison prioritizes recall first, precision second, and ROC-AUC third. When two scopes produce identical or effectively indistinguishable values under this priority, the narrower scope is reported because it preserves stronger biological specificity.

Table~\ref{tab:best-scope-by-antibiotic} reveals three interpretable groups among the machine-learning scope settings. Under this interpretation, \textit{all ML} is preferred for 6 antibiotics, \textit{broad ML} for 5, and \textit{strict ML} for 3. First, \textit{strict ML} is preferred for chloramphenicol, ciprofloxacin, and sulfisoxazole. These are cases where narrow determinant signatures are already highly informative. For ciprofloxacin, \textit{strict ML} reaches recall \(0.9917\) and precision \(0.9802\), while chloramphenicol reaches recall \(0.9846\) and precision \(0.9931\). In such antibiotics, widening the determinant pool does not improve the prioritized metrics enough to justify a less specific biological scope.

% Y doan: dien giai nhom antibiotic ma broad duoc uu tien.
% Vai tro: nhan manh broad huu ich khi within-class cross-resistance thong tin hon matching hep.
Second, \textit{broad} is preferred for ceftriaxone, ceftiofur, gentamicin, kanamycin, and streptomycin. This pattern is strongest for the beta-lactam tasks. For ceftriaxone, \textit{broad ML} reaches recall \(0.9761\) and precision \(0.9916\), compared with recall \(0.9101\) and precision \(0.2011\) under \textit{strict ML}. For ceftiofur, \textit{broad ML} reaches recall \(0.9760\) and precision \(0.9900\), compared with recall \(0.8842\) and precision \(0.2351\) under \textit{strict ML}. These cases indicate that within-class cross-resistance information is important and that narrow subclass-level matching is often too restrictive.

% Y doan: dien giai nhom antibiotic ma all duoc uu tien.
% Vai tro: cho thay khi nao maximum determinant coverage dem lai loi ich du doan manh nhat.
Third, \textit{all} is preferred for amoxicillin-clavulanic acid, ampicillin, cefoxitin, nalidixic acid, tetracycline, and trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. In these antibiotics, maximum determinant coverage gives the strongest final ranking. The clearest example is amoxicillin-clavulanic acid, where recall rises from \(0.1416\) under \textit{strict ML} to \(0.9521\) under \textit{broad ML} and \(0.9643\) under \textit{all ML}. Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole shows a similar pattern: \textit{all ML} reaches recall \(0.9713\), exceeding the \(0.9682\) reached by the narrower settings, while also maintaining ROC-AUC \(0.9935\).

\section{Comparison Between Rule-Based and Machine-Learning Prediction}

% Y doan: giai thich muc dich cua so sanh rule-based va ML.
% Vai tro: tach ro tac dong cua direct determinant matching khoi tac dong cua hoc cach ket hop evidence.
The contrast between rule-based and learned prediction distinguishes antibiotics for which direct determinant matching already captures most of the signal from those that require broader evidence to be combined more selectively.

% Y doan: chi ra cac truong hop direct rule da rat manh.
% Vai tro: chong lai cach hieu sai rang ML luc nao cung can thiet hon rule-based.
For some antibiotics, direct determinant matching is already very strong. Under \textit{strict rule-based} prediction, ciprofloxacin reaches precision \(0.9676\) and recall \(0.9983\), chloramphenicol reaches precision \(0.9897\) and recall \(0.9880\), and nalidixic acid reaches precision \(0.9527\) and recall \(0.9890\). These are exactly the cases where narrow mechanism-specific evidence already tracks the phenotype closely, so machine learning adds comparatively little beyond a direct biological rule.

% Y doan: chi ra khi direct rule tro nen qua tho va broad ML moi that su co gia tri.
% Vai tro: nhan manh dong gop thuc su cua ML trong bai toan nay.
For other antibiotics, however, direct rules are either too narrow or too coarse. Under \textit{strict rule-based} inference, ceftriaxone reaches precision only \(0.0904\) and recall \(0.1006\), while ceftiofur reaches precision \(0.1020\) and recall \(0.1328\). The learned strict models improve substantially on these same sparse representations, but they remain unstable, for example reaching only precision \(0.2011\) and recall \(0.9101\) for ceftriaxone. Under \textit{broad rule-based} matching, recall is recovered for many antibiotics, but precision collapses because any within-class determinant hit is treated as sufficient evidence. Ceftriaxone broad rule-based prediction reaches recall \(0.9852\) but precision only \(0.4894\); ceftiofur reaches recall \(0.9859\) but precision only \(0.4550\); gentamicin and kanamycin fall even lower in precision at \(0.2178\) and \(0.2808\), respectively.

This trade-off is easier to see when the rule-based results are viewed across antibiotics. The recall plot highlights where broader deterministic matching recovers resistant isolates more effectively, whereas the precision plot shows how often that same widening of scope leads to overcalling.

Figure~\ref{fig:rule-recall-by-scope} shows that \textit{broad rule-based} substantially improves recall over \textit{strict rule-based} for several antibiotics. In these cases, broader within-class matching recovers resistant isolates that are missed by the narrowest deterministic rule.

Figure~\ref{fig:rule-precision-by-scope} shows the corresponding cost in precision. The recall gain from broader deterministic matching often comes with a marked increase in false resistant calls, especially for ceftriaxone, ceftiofur, gentamicin, and kanamycin. In this setting, wider deterministic matching increases sensitivity, but it cannot distinguish between directly informative determinants and broader co-occurring evidence within the same class.

% Y doan: ket luan so sanh giua broad rule va broad ML.
% Vai tro: cho thay ML co gia tri nhat khi phai ket hop broad evidence mot cach co chon loc.
The most important gain from machine learning therefore appears when broader evidence must be combined selectively. For ceftriaxone, \textit{broad ML} reaches precision \(0.9916\) and recall \(0.9761\), preserving the sensitivity benefit of the broader evidence pool while avoiding the precision collapse of broad rule-based inference. The same pattern appears for ceftiofur, where \textit{broad ML} reaches precision \(0.9900\) and recall \(0.9760\). In short, the rule-based baselines show when direct matching is already enough, whereas the learned models are most valuable when multiple related determinants must be weighted rather than simply counted as hits.

\section{Feature Coverage and Zero-Feature Rows}

% Y doan: dinh nghia zero-feature row duoi workflow hien tai.
% Vai tro: bien day thanh mot phat hien thuc nghiem chinh thay vi mot chan doan phu.
Under the present workflow, a zero-feature row means that no retained genotype evidence remains for an isolate after scope filtering. In the rule-based setting this appears as a zero-hit row, while in the machine-learning setting it appears as an all-zero feature vector. This is not a minor diagnostic detail. It is one of the main mechanisms explaining why broader scopes often outperform the strict setting.

% Y doan: so sanh muc do zero-feature burden va feature coverage giua cac scope.
% Vai tro: noi truc tiep co che workflow voi chenh lech performance.
The difference across scopes is large. On average, \textit{strict} retains only \(32.6\) features per antibiotic and leaves \(4461.1\) zero-feature rows, while \textit{broad} increases average feature count to \(57.9\) and reduces the average zero-feature burden to \(3790.5\). The \textit{all} scope expands average feature count to \(377.4\) and removes the zero-feature problem entirely.

% Y doan: dien giai y nghia cua zero-feature burden.
% Vai tro: giai thich vi sao strict thuong yeu hon broad/all nhung van co mot so antibiotic manh.
This pattern explains much of the gap between \textit{strict} and the broader settings. A biologically conservative scope is attractive conceptually, but if it removes nearly all usable determinant evidence for large parts of the cohort, both direct rules and learned models are forced to work from a weak representation. At the same time, zero-feature burden is not the whole story. Strict still performs very well for antibiotics such as ciprofloxacin and chloramphenicol because the remaining narrow determinants are highly informative when they do appear. These results do not imply that strict matching is biologically wrong; they show that it is too sparse to serve as the strongest default across all antibiotics.

\section{Feature-Importance Interpretation}

% Y doan: dat khung cho feature importance.
% Vai tro: nhac nguoi doc rang importance la dau hieu du doan, khong phai bang chung co che nhan qua.
Feature-importance patterns indicate which retained determinants contribute most strongly to prediction under each scope. These rankings reflect predictive reliance rather than demonstrated causal mechanisms, but they help show how biological plausibility changes as the determinant-connection scope widens.

% Y doan: vi du strict.
% Vai tro: cho thay strict co the noi bat len cac determinant hep va giai thich duoc sinh hoc.
\paragraph{Strict scope: narrow mechanism-specific signals.}
For ciprofloxacin under \textit{strict}, the top-ranked features are dominated by quinolone-related mutations and genes, especially \texttt{gyrA\_D87Y}, \texttt{gyrA\_S83F}, and \texttt{qnrB19}. This is consistent with the strong strict-scope performance on ciprofloxacin and supports the interpretation that narrow determinant evidence can already be highly informative when the biological mechanism is direct and tightly linked to the target phenotype.

% Y doan: vi du broad.
% Vai tro: cho thay broad bat duoc cross-resistance trong cung family.
\paragraph{Broad scope: family-level cross-resistance becomes visible.}
For ceftriaxone under \textit{broad}, the highest-ranked features are led by beta-lactamase-related determinants such as \texttt{blaCTX-M-65} and \texttt{blaCMY-2}. This is exactly the biological pattern expected from the broad setting. The model benefits from within-family resistance information that would be too fragmented under a narrower subclass-level connection rule.

% Y doan: vi du all.
% Vai tro: cho thay all vua giu duoc signal hop ly vua de lo co-occurrence leakage.
\paragraph{All scope: strongest coverage, but mixed biological specificity.}
For amoxicillin-clavulanic acid under \textit{all}, highly plausible beta-lactamase-related features such as \texttt{blaCMY-2} remain dominant, but less directly related determinants such as \texttt{ant(2'')-Ia} and \texttt{aac(6')-Ib3} also rise in the ranking. A similar mixed pattern appears for trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, where expected \texttt{dfrA14} and \texttt{sul1}-related signals remain visible, but features such as \texttt{aadA2} and \texttt{blaCTX-M-15} also become predictive. These examples do not negate the predictive value of \textit{all}; rather, they clarify why the all-scope representation should be described as high-performing while remaining more vulnerable to co-occurrence leakage.

\section{Main Findings}

% Y doan: tong hop ket luan chinh cua chuong ket qua.
% Vai tro: khong lap lai tung bang/hinh, ma rut ra cac phat hien chinh tu bang chung da trinh bay.
The evaluation results support the following main findings:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item \textbf{Performance depends strongly on the determinant-connection scope.} Prediction quality changes substantially depending on whether determinant evidence is connected narrowly, broadly, or without biological restriction.
    \item \textbf{Scope effects vary substantially across antibiotics.} Because each model is fitted to one drug and one determinant profile, scope behavior is best interpreted at the antibiotic level rather than through aggregate scores alone.
    \item \textbf{Broad scope is the strongest biologically constrained setting.} It captures meaningful within-class cross-resistance signals and is especially effective for beta-lactam and aminoglycoside tasks.
    \item \textbf{All scope is the strongest global predictive default.} Its main advantages are complete feature coverage, no zero-feature rows, and the strongest overall ranking quality, although it also carries greater leakage risk.
    \item \textbf{Strict scope remains useful as a narrow biological reference.} It is not the strongest universal default, but it remains highly competitive for antibiotics whose phenotype is tightly linked to specific determinant signatures.
    \item \textbf{Machine learning adds most value when broader evidence must be combined selectively.} Direct rules can already be strong for some antibiotics, but they become too crude when wider within-class evidence is necessary.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion of Limitations}

% Y doan: neu ngan gon cac gioi han chinh cua danh gia hien tai.
% Vai tro: xac dinh pham vi cua ket qua ma khong lam loang ket luan chinh.
The present evaluation has several clear limitations. It predicts a binary resistant/susceptible phenotype rather than MIC, it relies on curated determinant representations rather than genome-wide features, and it has not yet been tested on an external validation cohort. The reported performance should therefore be interpreted as internal cross-validation performance rather than evidence of out-of-cohort generalization, because the stratified splits are not explicitly separated by lineage, source, geography, or collection time. In addition, feature-importance results indicate what the models found predictive, not what has been proven mechanistically causal. These limitations define the scope of the chapter. The results support a determinant-based analysis of genotype-to-phenotype connection behavior, but they should not yet be interpreted as a complete clinical validation study.
